Politics-Diet

Diet passes Imperial House Law revision for first time in 79 years, maintains male-only throne

Japan's Diet enacted a revision to the Imperial House Law — the first since 1947 — keeping the Chrysanthemum Throne restricted to male-line successors while adding provisions permitting sons of male-line adoptees to join the imperial family. Opposition scholars publicly disputed the LDP's constitutional framing of the male-only rule, and polling cited by Japan Times showed only 39% support for the adoptee-son pathway.

So what: The revision locks in a patrilineal framework at a moment when the imperial family's male-line membership is shrinking, foreclosing female-line or female emperor options for the foreseeable future.

Pattern: The last amendment to the Imperial House Law was enacted in 1947; the 2005 government panel recommended female-line succession, but that proposal was shelved after the birth of Prince Hisahito.

Nikkei slides over 4% in tech selloff; BOJ signals July rate hold as inflation expectations top 90%

The Nikkei 225 dropped more than 4% — briefly falling over 6% intraday — driven by a global technology selloff and Middle East conflict concerns, with chip stocks including Kioxia (whose market value has halved from its peak) among the hardest hit.
