US, South Korea, and Japan defense chiefs meet in Washington on North Korea threats

The defense ministers of the United States, South Korea, and Japan convened in Washington to coordinate responses to North Korea threats, per Korea JoongAng Daily. The meeting follows KCNA declarations of accelerated nuclear-force expansion and simultaneous reporting that Japan is internally weighing nuclear-option discussions under combined DPRK and China pressure.

So what: The trilateral defense chiefs meeting puts concrete institutional weight behind alliance coordination at a moment when DPRK messaging has explicitly targeted Japan's counterstrike capability program.

Pattern: US-Japan-ROK defense ministerial coordination on DPRK threats has intensified since North Korea's 2022 missile overflights of Japan and the 2023 Washington Declaration; today's Washington meeting continues that pattern.

Japan builds intelligence agency it hasn't had since World War II

Japan is constructing a new central intelligence agency — the first of its kind since World War II — with the Prime Minister's Office designated to lead intelligence coordination efforts, per the New York Times and Japan Times. The move formalizes Japan's counter-espionage response to documented Russian intelligence network expansion inside Japan and broader security-environment pressures.

So what: The new agency's structure — with the Prime Minister's Office at the lead — directly shapes how Japan integrates intelligence with the US alliance and Five Eyes partners.

Economy-BOJ

Takaichi's first economic policy blueprint puts defense industry revival in focus

PM Takaichi's first economic policy blueprint — released amid a bond market rout that pushed yields toward 1990s levels — explicitly prioritizes defense industry revival as a pillar of economic strategy, per the Japan Times. Takaichi publicly stated the blueprint is not responsible for the bond market movement, a claim she has now made repeatedly across multiple press cycles.

So what: The blueprint's defense-industry emphasis links the government's fiscal and industrial policy directly to Japan's 2%-of-GDP defense spending program and the Three Strategic Documents review underway in 2026.

Pattern: Japan's post-2022 defense spending ramp is the steepest since the postwar era; embedding defense industry revival in the economic blueprint is a structural integration not seen in prior Kishida-era documents.
