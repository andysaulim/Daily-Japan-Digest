Japan, US, South Korea issue joint statement of concern over China's South Pacific SLBM test

Japan, the United States, and South Korea jointly expressed concern over China's submarine-launched ballistic missile test into the South Pacific, with Chief Cabinet Secretary Kihara formally registering Tokyo's protest at his July 8 afternoon press conference. Japan simultaneously accelerated revision of its three key security documents — the National Security Strategy, National Defense Strategy, and Defense Buildup Program — in direct response to the test.

So what: The trilateral joint statement and the accelerated security document revision set a near-term baseline for Japan's diplomatic and defense posture ahead of any further Chinese strategic activity.

Pattern: China's SLBM test into the South Pacific, within waters covered by the Treaty of Rarotonga, follows China's July 6 test reported in the regional tracker; Japan's formal trilateral coordination mirrors the response framework used after prior North Korean missile provocations.

Jiji Press; NHK World; pna.gov.ph; asiae.co.kr

Japan's borrowing costs soar to 30-year high on debt fears

Japan's long-term borrowing costs surged to a 30-year high, driven by market concerns over fiscal sustainability, per the Financial Times. The Nikkei fell 2.78% on July 8 as higher oil prices — Brent up 9.22% to $78.42 — added to inflation concerns and compounded selling pressure.

So what: Rising JGB yields at a 30-year high directly pressure the BOJ's normalization timeline and Japan's fiscal space for the defense spending buildup toward 2% of GDP by FY2027.

Financial Times; Mainichi (Tokyo stocks)
China-Japan

China and Japan trade conflicting accounts of confrontation around Senkaku Islands

China and Japan issued contradictory versions of a confrontation in waters surrounding the Senkaku / Diaoyu Islands, per Asahi Shimbun, while China's Global Times separately rebuked Tokyo for what Beijing characterized as interference with Chinese scientific research operations near the Diaoyu Dao. The dueling narratives follow the July 7 entry of two China Coast Guard ships into Japanese territorial waters off the Senkakus documented in the regional tracker.

So what: The competing official narratives raise the baseline for diplomatic friction management at a moment when Japan is simultaneously responding to China's SLBM test.

Pattern: China's use of 'scientific research' as a justification for operations near the Senkakus echoes a pattern of dual-use maritime activity documented in the East China Sea incident timeline going back to 2016.

A weak yen spells trouble for Japan—at home and abroad
The yen's sustained weakness — USD/JPY at 162.54 — generates structural problems for Japan's economy domestically through import-cost inflation and externally through trade friction and competitiveness distortions.
The Atlantic Council analysis argues that the weak yen is not simply a BOJ policy artifact but a signal of deeper imbalances creating dual risks: domestically, higher import costs for energy and food that suppress household real incomes; and abroad, friction with trading partners, including the US, over currency-driven export advantages. The piece is directly relevant as USD/JPY holds at 162.54 and household spending has declined for a sixth consecutive month in May.
