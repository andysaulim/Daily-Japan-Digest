Defense

China test-fires SLBM from nuclear submarine into South Pacific nuclear-free zone; Japan voices concern and accelerates security document revision

China test-launched a submarine-launched ballistic missile into the South Pacific, an area covered by the Treaty of Rarotonga nuclear-free zone, prompting Japan, Australia, and New Zealand to formally object. Chief Cabinet Secretary Kihara addressed the test at both his morning and afternoon press conferences on July 7, and reporting from Korea's Asia Economy outlet states Japan is accelerating revision of its three key National Security Strategy documents in response.

So what: Acceleration of Japan's NSS revision in direct response to the SLBM test would represent the first documented link between a specific Chinese strategic weapons demonstration and a formal Japanese security document cycle.

Pattern: China notified Japan of the test in advance per Kyodo, echoing a notification procedure last used during China's 1980 ICBM test; the South Pacific nuclear-free zone angle adds a Treaty of Rarotonga compliance dimension not present in prior East China Sea or Taiwan Strait incidents.

China-Japan

Two China Coast Guard ships enter Japanese territorial waters off Senkaku Islands

Two China Coast Guard vessels entered Japanese territorial waters off the Senkaku Islands, per Jiji Press, extending a pattern of CCG intrusions that has drawn repeated MOFA protests in 2026. China's Global Times simultaneously published a rebuke of what Beijing characterized as Japanese interference with Chinese scientific research operations in waters surrounding the Diaoyu Dao (Beijing's name for the Senkakus).

So what: The concurrent SLBM test protest and Senkaku CCG intrusion on the same day mark the first time in the current regional tracker period that Japan has had to lodge overlapping sea and strategic-weapons protests against China within a single 24-hour window.

Pattern: China's Coast Guard has maintained a near-daily presence in the Senkaku contiguous zone since 2016; today's territorial-water entry follows a July 2 CCG order to a Japanese survey vessel to halt EEZ operations, per tracker data.

Economy-BOJ

Japan real wages rise 1.4% in May; nominal wages top 3% in longest streak since 1992

Japan's real wages climbed 1.4 percent year-on-year in May while nominal wages exceeded 3 percent growth for the longest consecutive streak since 1992, per Jiji Press data reported by the Financial Times. The FT's Leo Lewis-bylined piece states the data bolsters the Bank of Japan's policy normalization path.

So what: Sustained real wage growth above 1 percent is the threshold BOJ Governor Ueda has cited as necessary for confidence that the 2-percent inflation target is durably met; the data feeds directly into the next BOJ policy meeting assessment.

Pattern: Japan's nominal wage growth has now exceeded 3 percent for the longest streak since 1992, a period that followed the collapse of the asset-price bubble; the current streak reflects post-pandemic shunto wage negotiations rather than asset inflation.
